Urban Stress Across the G7: Major Cities, Housing Pressure and Fertility

The G7 countries are among the wealthiest economies in the world. Yet their largest cities show striking differences in structural cost pressure.

Using the Urban Stress Index (USI) — defined as:

USI = Rental Index + Engel’s Index
(housing and essential food cost as % of median gross income)

We examine four dimensions:

  1. Top 10 highest USI cities
  2. Top 10 highest food burden cities
  3. Top 10 highest rental burden cities
  4. Rental burden in the most populous city vs national fertility rate (TFR)

1. The Top 10 Highest USI Cities

The highest USI scores are heavily concentrated in:

  • 🇮🇹 Southern Italy
  • 🇨🇦 Multiple Canadian cities
  • 🇬🇧 Parts of the UK

Naples leads the ranking with an exceptionally high structural burden. In this case, both food share and rental share are elevated, pushing total baseline cost pressure close to 90% of median gross income.

Canadian cities also cluster at the top — not because of food costs, but because rental burden remains persistently high across several metros.

Key pattern:

High USI is primarily driven by rent, not food.

2. Top 10 Food Burden Cities (Engel’s Index)

Food burden shows far less dispersion than rental burden.

The highest Engel’s Index values appear in:

  • 🇮🇹 Naples
  • 🇨🇦 Halifax
  • 🇨🇦 Vancouver

However, even the highest food shares range roughly between 15–24%. Compared to rental shares (often exceeding 50%), food costs are relatively stable across advanced economies.

Interpretation:

Food matters — but it is rarely the dominant driver of structural urban stress in developed cities.

3. Top 10 Rental Burden Cities

This is where the divergence becomes dramatic.

Rental shares exceeding 50% of median income appear in:

  • 🇮🇹 Naples
  • 🇨🇦 Halifax
  • 🇨🇦 Vancouver
  • 🇮🇹 Rome
  • 🇨🇦 Toronto

Once rental burden crosses 50%, total USI often enters the “Extreme” category.

At that threshold, median earners have limited flexibility before accounting for:

  • Savings
  • Childcare
  • Healthcare
  • Taxes

Conclusion from the first three charts:

Urban stress in G7 cities is overwhelmingly a housing-driven phenomenon.

Food contributes — but housing determines tier classification.

4. Rental Burden vs Fertility (TFR)

The final chart compares:

  • Rental Index of the most populous city in each country
  • National Total Fertility Rate (TFR)

At first glance, one might expect a negative relationship:

Higher rental burden → Lower fertility.

However, the data shows no clear linear pattern.

Examples:

  • 🇮🇹 Italy — High rental burden, low TFR
  • 🇨🇦 Canada — High rental burden, low TFR
  • 🇯🇵 Japan — Moderate rental burden, very low TFR
  • 🇫🇷 France — Moderate rental burden, relatively higher TFR
  • 🇺🇸 USA — Moderate rental burden, mid-range TFR

The relationship appears suggestive in some cases, but inconsistent overall.

Important clarification:

USI measures structural cost pressure relative to median income. Fertility outcomes depend on many additional variables:

  • Family policy
  • Childcare subsidies
  • Cultural norms
  • Tax systems
  • Labor market flexibility

Housing stress may influence household decisions — but it does not mechanically determine fertility rates.

Overall Findings

  • Structural stress varies widely across major G7 cities.
  • Housing burden dominates total USI variation.
  • Food burden is relatively stable.
  • Fertility does not exhibit a simple correlation with rental pressure.

The richest economies do not necessarily offer the lowest structural urban stress.

And while high housing burden may constrain household formation, demographic outcomes depend on broader institutional context.
