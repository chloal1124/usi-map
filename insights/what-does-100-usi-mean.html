What Does a 100 USI Mean?

Naples currently records a USI of 88.41.

That is already extremely high.

You can see how it compares across major G7 cities here:

Urban Stress Across the G7: Major Cities, Housing Pressure and Fertility →

But if 88 feels extreme, the real question is this:

What would a USI of 100 actually mean?

And is it even possible?

First, What Is USI Measuring?

USI is simply: 

Housing share of income + essential food share of income

It measures how much of a median earner’s gross salary is absorbed by two basic things:

  • Rent
  • Food

It does not include tax. It does not include transport. It does not include savings, childcare, insurance, or lifestyle.

It only asks one question:

How much of your income disappears before anything else even starts?

So What Does 100 Mean?

A USI of 100 means this:

If you earn the median income in that city, 100 percent of your gross salary goes to rent and basic food.

Before tax. Before utilities. Before everything else.

There is no room left.

That is no longer simply “expensive.” It means the market baseline and median wages no longer match.

Can USI Go Above 100?

Yes.

And when it does, the math becomes uncomfortable.

Imagine this:

  • Median monthly income: 3,000
  • Monthly rent (1 bedroom): 3,200
  • Basic monthly food cost: 400

Rent alone is already 106 percent of income. Food adds another 13 percent.

USI becomes 120.

That means a median earner cannot live alone at market rent, even before tax.

Something has to change:

  • They share a flat
  • They live with family
  • They move far outside the city
  • They earn above median

The system adjusts, but the pressure is real.

Where Do Most Cities Sit?

In most advanced cities, USI falls between 30 and 50.

That range usually means:

  • Rent under 40 percent of income
  • Food between 8 and 15 percent

Cities in this range may feel expensive, but they are structurally manageable.

When USI moves into the 60 to 80 range, pressure becomes visible. Savings slow down. Independence becomes tighter.

When it approaches 100, independence itself becomes difficult at median income.

What Would a City Near 100 Look Like?

It would not look chaotic.

It would look normal on the surface.

But you would see:

  • More flat sharing
  • More adults living with parents
  • Delayed household formation
  • Longer commuting distances

People adapt.

But adaptation is not the same as affordability.
