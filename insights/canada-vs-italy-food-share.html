Canada vs Italy: Why Does Italy’s Food Share Look Higher?

In the current dataset, several Canadian cities show Engel values around 18, while parts of Italy approach 24.

At face value, that implies food absorbs a larger share of income in Italy. However, methodology matters.

The Restaurant Proxy Limitation

Version one of the index estimates food cost using the price of an inexpensive restaurant meal.

Restaurant prices embed:

  • Labor
  • Commercial rent
  • Tourism premiums
  • Location effects

In tourism-heavy cities such as Rome, Milan, or Naples, restaurant pricing may exceed grocery-level affordability.

Thus, part of Italy’s higher Engel component reflects proxy construction rather than pure supermarket cost.

Why Canada Appears Lower

Canada’s food pricing is not necessarily cheap. However, higher median wages reduce the share relative to income.

USI measures proportion — not sticker price alone.

Methodology Upgrade

To reduce distortion, the index is transitioning toward a grocery-based reference:

→ The Chicken Pasta Index

By focusing on supermarket ingredients rather than menus, the food component becomes:

  • More comparable
  • Less tourism-sensitive
  • Closer to everyday budgeting

Conclusion

Italy’s higher Engel value reflects structural income differences and proxy choice. It does not automatically imply grocery prices are higher than in Canada.

Method refinement improves interpretability — not the underlying structural comparison.
