Cost of Living and Disposable Income in San Francisco

Urban Stress Index: 22.95 (Comfortable)

The cost of living in San Francisco relative to income can be understood using the Urban Stress Index (USI). In San Francisco, housing absorbs about 18.4% of a typical monthly income, while essential food costs account for roughly 4.5%. After rent and food, a typical earner retains around 77.1% of income for other living expenses, savings, and discretionary spending.

Methodology

The Urban Stress Index (USI) measures the share of a typical monthly income dispensed as essential housing and food costs for a single person with a local full time job. It combines two core components:

  • Housing burden – calculated as monthly rent for a one-bedroom apartment divided by typical monthly income.
  • Essential food share – calculated as the estimated cost of a standardized essential dining pattern divided by typical monthly income.

USI is defined as:
USI = Housing burden + Essential food share

All values are expressed as percentages of monthly income in local currency. The index is designed to provide a comparable indicator of cost-of-living pressure across cities, focusing on baseline expenses.

Cost Structure Analysis

San Francisco remains one of the most expensive housing markets in the United States, with rents among the highest in North America. However, the city also benefits from extremely high income levels driven by the broader Silicon Valley technology economy.

Despite high absolute housing prices, the strong wage environment significantly reduces the relative financial burden on households. As a result, San Francisco records a USI of roughly 23, indicating considerably lower affordability stress than many Canadian cities such as Vancouver or Toronto, where housing costs absorb a much larger share of income.

Similar to San Jose, the relatively low USI in San Francisco is partly influenced by exceptionally high salaries in the technology sector. Income levels vary widely across occupations, and workers outside the technology industry may face significantly higher housing pressure than suggested by the city’s median income. This structural income gap within technology-driven cities is explored further in our upcoming analysis (coming soon).

Interestingly, service-sector wages in Silicon Valley can also be substantially higher than in many other cities. In some cases, supervisory roles in hospitality or retail may earn incomes comparable to certain professional occupations in Canadian cities such as Vancouver. Sources

Income figures are based on nationally reported typical or median monthly earnings. Rental data reflects prevailing market rates for one-bedroom apartments within the metropolitan area.

Food costs are derived from standardized essential grocery basket estimates designed to represent a lifestyle of occasional dining-out in the local area.

All data are compiled from publicly available national statistics agencies, rental market listings, and aggregated consumer price datasets.
