Cost of Living and Disposable Income in Calgary

Urban Stress Index: 55.73 (Extreme)

The cost of living in Calgary relative to income can be understood using the Urban Stress Index (USI). In Calgary, housing absorbs about 39.2% of a typical monthly income, while essential food costs account for roughly 16.6%. After rent and food, a typical earner retains around 44.3% of income for other living expenses, savings, and discretionary spending.

Methodology

The Urban Stress Index (USI) measures the share of a typical monthly income dispensed as essential housing and food costs for a single person with a local full time job. It combines two core components:

  • Housing burden – calculated as monthly rent for a one-bedroom apartment divided by typical monthly income.
  • Essential food share – calculated as the estimated cost of a standardized essential dining pattern divided by typical monthly income.

USI is defined as:
USI = Housing burden + Essential food share

All values are expressed as percentages of monthly income in local currency. The index is designed to provide a comparable indicator of cost-of-living pressure across cities, focusing on baseline expenses.

Cost Structure Analysis

Food expenditure in Canadian cities typically accounts for around 15–18% of household income, forming a relatively high baseline cost compared with many other developed economies.

Compared with major metropolitan areas such as Toronto or Vancouver, housing costs in Calgary are noticeably lower. Rent for a typical one-bedroom apartment is significantly cheaper despite average salaries being broadly similar across these cities.

However, affordability pressures remain meaningful. Even with cheaper rents, Calgary’s Urban Stress Index still reaches around 55, meaning that more than half of a typical gross salary is consumed by housing and food alone. Compared with its provincial neighbour Edmonton, Calgary remains more financially constrained (55 vs 42), although both cities benefit from the relatively strong wage environment associated with the regional energy sector. Similar dynamics can also be observed in energy-driven cities such as Austin in the United States.

Sources

Income figures are based on nationally reported typical or median monthly earnings. Rental data reflects prevailing market rates for one-bedroom apartments within the metropolitan area.

Food costs are derived from standardized essential grocery basket estimates designed to represent a lifestyle of occasional dining-out in the local area.

All data are compiled from publicly available national statistics agencies, rental market listings, and aggregated consumer price datasets.
