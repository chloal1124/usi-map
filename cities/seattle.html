Cost of Living and Disposable Income in Seattle

Urban Stress Index: 26.55 (Comfortable)

The cost of living in Seattle relative to income can be understood using the Urban Stress Index (USI). In Seattle, housing absorbs about 19.5% of a typical monthly income, while essential food costs account for roughly 7.0%. After rent and food, a typical earner retains around 73.4% of income for other living expenses, savings, and discretionary spending.

Methodology

The Urban Stress Index (USI) measures the share of a typical monthly income dispensed as essential housing and food costs for a single person with a local full time job. It combines two core components:

  • Housing burden – calculated as monthly rent for a one-bedroom apartment divided by typical monthly income.
  • Essential food share – calculated as the estimated cost of a standardized essential dining pattern divided by typical monthly income.

USI is defined as:
USI = Housing burden + Essential food share

All values are expressed as percentages of monthly income in local currency. The index is designed to provide a comparable indicator of cost-of-living pressure across cities, focusing on baseline expenses.

Cost Structure Analysis

Seattle is one of the major technology hubs in North America, supported by large technology employers and a strong knowledge-based economy. As a result, average income levels are significantly higher than in many other metropolitan areas, helping offset the city’s relatively high housing costs.

Despite rising rents in recent years, Seattle records a USI of roughly 26, indicating moderate affordability pressure compared with other major coastal cities. This places Seattle between the extreme salary-driven affordability of San Jose and San Francisco, and more typical high-income urban economies such as Boston.

A particularly interesting comparison can be made with nearby Vancouver. Although the two cities share similar geographic settings and housing pressures, Seattle’s substantially higher wage levels result in far lower overall affordability stress. Vancouver records a USI above 70, highlighting the stark differences in wage structures across North American labour markets.

As with other technology-driven cities, it should also be noted that the relatively low USI in Seattle partly reflects the influence of high salaries in the technology sector. Income levels vary widely across occupations, and service-sector workers may experience significantly higher housing pressure than suggested by the city’s median income. Sources

Income figures are based on nationally reported typical or median monthly earnings. Rental data reflects prevailing market rates for one-bedroom apartments within the metropolitan area.

Food costs are derived from standardized essential grocery basket estimates designed to represent a lifestyle of occasional dining-out in the local area.

All data are compiled from publicly available national statistics agencies, rental market listings, and aggregated consumer price datasets.
