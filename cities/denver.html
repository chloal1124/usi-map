Cost of Living and Disposable Income in Denver

Urban Stress Index: 28.02 (Comfortable)

The cost of living in Denver relative to income can be understood using the Urban Stress Index (USI). In Denver, housing absorbs about 20.7% of a typical monthly income, while essential food costs account for roughly 7.3%. After rent and food, a typical earner retains around 72.0% of income for other living expenses, savings, and discretionary spending.

Methodology

The Urban Stress Index (USI) measures the share of a typical monthly income dispensed as essential housing and food costs for a single person with a local full time job. It combines two core components:

  • Housing burden – calculated as monthly rent for a one-bedroom apartment divided by typical monthly income.
  • Essential food share – calculated as the estimated cost of a standardized essential dining pattern divided by typical monthly income.

USI is defined as:
USI = Housing burden + Essential food share

All values are expressed as percentages of monthly income in local currency. The index is designed to provide a comparable indicator of cost-of-living pressure across cities, focusing on baseline expenses.

Cost Structure Analysis

Denver offers a relatively balanced affordability profile, supported by a diversified economy and moderate food costs. Compared with nearby Boulder, everyday living costs are slightly less pressured by housing scarcity, although affordability has become more constrained in recent years.

Housing remains the primary driver of stress. Sustained population growth and strong demand have pushed rents upward, though the city remains more affordable than major coastal metropolitan areas. Within the Mountain West, Denver sits between the higher-cost structure of Boulder and the slightly lighter housing burden seen in Salt Lake City.

Overall, Denver’s USI reflects a stretched but still manageable cost structure, with housing pressure rising faster than other components of daily living.

Sources

Income figures are based on nationally reported typical or median monthly earnings. Rental data reflects prevailing market rates for one-bedroom apartments within the metropolitan area.

Food costs are derived from standardized essential grocery basket estimates designed to represent a lifestyle of occasional dining-out in the local area.

All data are compiled from publicly available national statistics agencies, rental market listings, and aggregated consumer price datasets.
