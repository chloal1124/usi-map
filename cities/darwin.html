Cost of Living and Disposable Income in Darwin

Urban Stress Index: 39.54 (High burden)

The cost of living in Darwin relative to income can be understood using the Urban Stress Index (USI). In Darwin, housing absorbs about 27.1% of a typical monthly income, while essential food costs account for roughly 12.4%. After rent and food, a typical earner retains around 60.5% of income for other living expenses, savings, and discretionary spending.

Methodology

The Urban Stress Index (USI) measures the share of a typical monthly income dispensed as essential housing and food costs for a single person with a local full time job. It combines two core components:

  • Housing burden – calculated as monthly rent for a one-bedroom apartment divided by typical monthly income.
  • Essential food share – calculated as the estimated cost of a standardized essential dining pattern divided by typical monthly income.

USI is defined as:
USI = Housing burden + Essential food share

All values are expressed as percentages of monthly income in local currency. The index is designed to provide a comparable indicator of cost-of-living pressure across cities, focusing on baseline expenses.

Cost Structure Analysis

Darwin records the highest food expenditure share among Australian cities, exceeding 12% of gross income. This largely reflects the city's geographic isolation, where higher transport and logistics costs increase grocery and restaurant prices.

Despite these elevated living costs, Darwin’s wage levels remain relatively strong compared with other Australian cities. As a result, the overall affordability pressure remains moderate. Darwin records a USI of roughly 40, broadly comparable to the level observed in Sydney.

A useful comparison can be made with Halifax, another relatively small and geographically remote city. While both cities face higher food costs due to transport constraints, Halifax records a much higher USI of around 75. This contrast highlights how stronger wages in Darwin help offset higher living costs, resulting in a far more balanced affordability outcome.

Sources

Income figures are based on nationally reported typical or median monthly earnings. Rental data reflects prevailing market rates for one-bedroom apartments within the metropolitan area.

Food costs are derived from standardized essential grocery basket estimates designed to represent a lifestyle of occasional dining-out in the local area.

All data are compiled from publicly available national statistics agencies, rental market listings, and aggregated consumer price datasets.
