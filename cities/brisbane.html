Cost of Living and Disposable Income in Brisbane

Urban Stress Index: 37.62 (High burden)

The cost of living in Brisbane relative to income can be understood using the Urban Stress Index (USI). In Brisbane, housing absorbs about 29.0% of a typical monthly income, while essential food costs account for roughly 8.6%. After rent and food, a typical earner retains around 62.4% of income for other living expenses, savings, and discretionary spending.

Methodology

The Urban Stress Index (USI) measures the share of a typical monthly income dispensed as essential housing and food costs for a single person with a local full time job. It combines two core components:

  • Housing burden – calculated as monthly rent for a one-bedroom apartment divided by typical monthly income.
  • Essential food share – calculated as the estimated cost of a standardized essential dining pattern divided by typical monthly income.

USI is defined as:
USI = Housing burden + Essential food share

All values are expressed as percentages of monthly income in local currency. The index is designed to provide a comparable indicator of cost-of-living pressure across cities, focusing on baseline expenses.

Cost Structure Analysis

Food expenditure in Brisbane represents roughly 8–9% of gross income, remaining broadly consistent with other Australian cities. Strong wage levels across the Australian labour market help keep everyday living costs relatively manageable compared with many North American cities.

Housing costs, however, have increased significantly in recent years. Brisbane was historically considered one of Australia's more affordable metropolitan markets, but rapid population growth and interstate migration have pushed rents upward. As a result, Brisbane now records a USI of roughly 38, exceeding the levels observed in Melbourne and Perth.

A comparable pattern can be observed in fast-growing metropolitan areas such as Phoenix in the United States, where strong population growth has also driven housing demand. However, Phoenix still records a lower USI of around 28, reflecting lower food costs and a lighter overall housing burden relative to income.

Sources

Income figures are based on nationally reported typical or median monthly earnings. Rental data reflects prevailing market rates for one-bedroom apartments within the metropolitan area.

Food costs are derived from standardized essential grocery basket estimates designed to represent a lifestyle of occasional dining-out in the local area.

All data are compiled from publicly available national statistics agencies, rental market listings, and aggregated consumer price datasets.
